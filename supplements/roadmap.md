# Supplementary Materials Roadmap

A comprehensive outline of enchiridia, exercise sets, lab manuals, notation guides,
and study aids needed across the curriculum. Organized by period, with cross-cutting
conceptual tracks noted.

**Types of supplement:**
- **Enchiridion** — compact handbook bridging the original text to modern understanding
- **Exercise set** — problems, proofs, or coding challenges
- **Lab manual** — hands-on experiments with materials lists and safety notes
- **Notation guide** — translating archaic or unfamiliar notation to modern equivalents
- **Context note** — brief "why we're reading this now" orientation (1–2 pages max)

---

## 1. Ancient Greece

### Mathematics

**Euclid — *Elements***
- Notation guide: Euclidean geometric language → modern notation (e.g., "the square on AB" → AB²)
- Exercise set: Reproduce key constructions with compass and straightedge (Books I–IV)
- Exercise set: Prove selected propositions independently before reading Euclid's proofs
- Enchiridion: Guide to the axiomatic method — what are postulates, common notions, and why this structure matters
- Enchiridion: Book V (theory of proportion) bridge to modern ratio/real number concepts
- Enchiridion: Books XI–XIII spatial geometry — guide to visualizing 3D constructions

**Apollonius — *Conic Sections***
- Notation guide: Apollonius's construction-based definitions → modern analytic equations (parabola, ellipse, hyperbola)
- Context note: Why conics matter — from Greek curiosity to Kepler's orbits to satellite dishes
- Exercise set: Derive the standard equation of each conic from Apollonius's geometric definitions

**Archimedes — *Works***
- Enchiridion: Archimedes' method of exhaustion as proto-calculus — connection to limits and integration
- Lab manual: Buoyancy and density experiments (crown problem, floating bodies)
- Lab manual: Lever and center of gravity experiments
- Exercise set: Compute areas and volumes using Archimedean methods, then compare with calculus
- Context note: The palimpsest and *The Method* — what Archimedes knew about infinitesimals

### Astronomy

**Ptolemy — *Almagest***
- Enchiridion: Ptolemaic model explained — epicycles, deferents, equants with diagrams
- Notation guide: Ptolemy's chord tables → modern trigonometry (chord → sine relationship)
- Exercise set: Predict planetary positions using simplified Ptolemaic models
- Context note: Why this model worked so well for 1400 years

### Philosophy & Natural Philosophy

**Pre-Socratics (fragments)**
- Context note: Who the pre-Socratics were, why fragments survive, how to read fragmentary evidence
- Enchiridion: Atomic theory thread — Democritus → Epicurus → Lucretius → Dalton → modern

**Plato — *Meno*, *Timaeus*, *Theaetetus*, *Phaedrus*, *Symposium***
- Context note: Plato's epistemology and its relevance to mathematical knowledge (*Meno*'s slave boy)
- Context note: *Timaeus* as mathematical cosmology — Platonic solids, the Demiurge, relation to physics
- Study questions for each dialogue (not answers — prompts for reflection)

**Aristotle — *Physics*, *Metaphysics*, *Nicomachean Ethics*, *Categories*, *Politics***

- Context note: Why Aristotle's physics was wrong but rational — and what it takes to overturn a paradigm

- Study questions for the *Ethics* and *Politics*

### Medicine

**Hippocrates — *Genuine Works***
- Context note: Hippocratic method as early empiricism — observation, prognosis, case recording
- Enchiridion: Humoral theory — what it got wrong and what it got right (systematic diagnosis)

### Drama

### Language

**Ancient Greek**

## Supplement Format Standards

Each supplement should follow consistent formatting:

- **Title** and the text(s) it accompanies
- **Prerequisites** — what the student should have read/completed first
- **Learning objectives** — what the student will understand or be able to do
- **Content** — the actual guide, exercises, or lab instructions
- **Connections** — explicit links to other parts of the curriculum ("revisit when reading X")
- **Sources** — references for further reading (not required, but available)

Exercise sets should include:
- Difficulty ratings (1–3 stars)
- Selected answers (not all — some should require independent verification)
- Historical context for each problem where relevant

Lab manuals should include:
- Materials list with approximate costs and sourcing
- Safety notes
- Step-by-step procedures
- Expected results and troubleshooting
- Questions for reflection connecting the experiment to the readings
