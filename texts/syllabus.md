# Open Great Books Program - Curriculum Overview

## Program Philosophy

A "Great Books" style education combining:
- Classical liberal arts tradition (primary texts, minimal editorialization)
- Practical computer science and engineering skills
- Mathematical and scientific foundations from first principles
- Philosophical and humanistic context

**Core Principle:** Let texts speak for themselves; curation through juxtaposition rather than framing.

**Outcome:** Students who complete the "grand tour" will:
- Have read foundational texts from Euclid to modern computing
- Possess practical skills in mathematics, algorithms, and systems programming
- Understand the historical/philosophical context of technical work
- Be prepared for technical careers or specialized further study

---

## Program Structure

### Grand Tour (2-3 years, full-time equivalent)
The complete chronological journey with deep engagement.

### Focused Tracks (6-12 months)
Curated paths through specific areas, for example:
- Mathematical Foundations
- Computing Systems
- Algorithms & Theory
- Physics & Natural Philosophy

### Quick Dives (1-2 months)
Concentrated explorations:
- History of Computation
- Boolean Logic from First Principles
- Specific philosophical questions

## Chronological Curriculum Outline

### 1. Ancient Greece (Foundations)

**Mathematics:**
- Euclid - *Elements* (most of it, especially Books I-VI, XI-XIII)
- Apollonius - *Treatise on Conic Sections*
- Archimedes - Works on geometry, mechanics, hydrostatics

**Astronomy:**
- Ptolemy - *Almagest* (selections on epicycles, astronomical models)

**Philosophy & Natural Philosophy:**
- Pre-Socratics - Fragments (Heraclitus, Parmenides, Democritus - atomic theory)
- Plato - *Meno*, *Timaeus*, *Theaetetus*, *Phaedrus* (epistemology, metaphysics, theory of mind)
- Aristotle - *Physics*, *Metaphysics*, *Nicomachean Ethics*, *Organon* (logic), biological works

**Medicine:**
- Hippocrates - *Hippocratic Corpus* (selections)

**Language & Culture:**
- Ancient Greek grammar and translation exercises
- Greek drama - Sophocles (*Antigone*), Aeschylus (*Oresteia*), Aristophanes (for cultural context)

**Practical Components:**
- Euclidean geometric proofs by hand
- Archimedes' experiments (buoyancy, density, levers)
- Derivation of physical units

---

### 2. Rome & Late Antiquity

**Engineering & Architecture:**
- Vitruvius - *De Architectura*

**Philosophy & Poetry:**
- Lucretius - *De Rerum Natura* (Epicurean atomism)
- Roman engineering (aqueducts, concrete, roads) - supplementary materials

**Early Christian Thought:**
- Augustine, Aquinas, Anselm (selections showing Greek influence on medieval thought)

---

### 3. Islamic Golden Age & Medieval Europe

**Islamic Contributions (Essential for mathematical continuity):**
- Al-Khwarizmi - *Al-Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr wa'l-muqābala* (algebra, algorithms)
- Ibn al-Haytham - *Book of Optics* (experimental method, optics)
- Omar Khayyam - Work on cubic equations and geometric algebra

**Medieval European:**
- Fibonacci - *Liber Abaci* (Hindu-Arabic numerals, algebra in Europe)
- Roger Bacon - Experimental philosophy and optics
- Nicole Oresme - Early graphing, proto-calculus concepts
- Scholastic logic (Aquinas, Ockham)
- Hildegard von Bingen - Monophony, Medicine
- Port-Royal Logic

**Music Theory:**
- Mathematical foundations of harmony, counterpoint, rhythm
- Composition of melody
- Monophony and Polyphony

**Practical Components:**
- Algebra exercises begin here
- Translation and understanding of mathematical notation evolution

---

### 4. Renaissance & Scientific Revolution (1500-1700)

**Mathematical Revolution:**
- François Viète - Symbolic algebra
- René Descartes - *La Géométrie* (analytic geometry)
- Pierre de Fermat - Number theory, early calculus (tangents, maxima/minima)
- Blaise Pascal - *Traité du triangle arithmétique*, probability theory, projective geometry
- John Wallis - Infinite series, interpolation

**New Philosophy & Method:**
- Francis Bacon - *Novum Organum* (scientific method)
- René Descartes - *Discourse on Method*, *Meditations*
- Antoine Arnauld - *Logic, or The Art of Thinking*

**Experimental Natural Philosophy:**
- Galileo Galilei - *Dialogues Concerning Two New Sciences*, *Starry Messenger* (kinematics, astronomy)
- Johannes Kepler - Planetary laws, *Astronomiae Pars Optica*, *Dioptrice*
- Christiaan Huygens - Pendulums, wave theory of light, collision laws
- Robert Hooke - *Micrographia* (microscopy, wave theory)
- Robert Boyle - *The Sceptical Chymist* (moving beyond alchemy)

**Optics Thread (Conceptual):**
- Kepler - How lenses work
- Snell/Descartes - Refraction laws
- Grimaldi - Diffraction
- Hooke - Wave theory
- Huygens - Wave theory development
- Newton - *Opticks* (particle theory, color experiments)

**Practical Components:**
- Working through Fermat's method of maxima
- Descartes' analytic geometry exercises
- Galileo's falling body calculations
- Replicating optical experiments

---

### 5. The Newtonian Synthesis & Enlightenment (1687-1800)

**Physics & Mathematics:**
- Isaac Newton - *Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica*, *Opticks*
- Gottfried Leibniz - Calculus, philosophy, binary arithmetic, *calculus ratiocinator* concept
- Leonhard Euler - Numerous contributions (analysis, number theory, mechanics)
- Bernoulli family - Jakob (*Ars Conjectandi* - probability), others
- Blaise Pascal - *Treaties on the Arithmetic Triangle* (combinatorics, mathematical induction)

**Probability & Statistics Thread:**
- Cardano - *Liber de Ludo Aleae* (gambling probability)
- Pascal & Fermat - Correspondence on probability
- Huygens - *De Ratiociniis in Ludo Aleae*
- Jakob Bernoulli - *Ars Conjectandi* (law of large numbers)
- Abraham de Moivre - Normal distribution
- Thomas Bayes - Bayesian inference (posthumous 1763)
- Pierre-Simon Laplace - *Théorie Analytique des Probabilités* (synthesis, central limit theorem)

**Chemistry Thread Begins:**
- Robert Boyle (already mentioned above)
- Antoine Lavoisier - Chemical revolution, conservation of mass, oxygen theory
- Joseph Priestley - Discovery of oxygen, gases
- John Dalton - Modern atomic theory with experimental basis
- Amedeo Avogadro - Molecular theory, gas laws
- Michael Faraday - Electrochemistry (comes slightly later, ~1830s)

**Philosophy:**
- Blaise Pascal - *Pensées* (sections on existentialism, lack of universality in mathematics)
- David Hume - *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* (empiricism, causation, induction problem)
- Immanuel Kant - *Critique of Pure Reason* (read alongside Newton to understand epistemological foundations)
- Jean-Jacques Rousseau - *Discourse on the Arts and Sciences* (provokes reflection on technological progress)
- The Federalist Papers - sections on Progressive Era politics


**Practical Components:**
- Deriving Newton's laws and working through *Principia* proofs (geometric methods)
- Translating Newton's geometric calculus into modern notation
- Newton's fundamental derivation of calculus by hand
- Extensive calculus practice
- Chemical experiments (where safe and practical)

---

### 6. 19th Century - Expansion & Specialization (1800-1900)

**Electricity & Magnetism:**
- Benjamin Franklin - Static electricity experiments
- Charles-Augustin de Coulomb - Inverse square law for electric force
- Luigi Galvani - Animal electricity
- Alessandro Volta - Voltaic pile (chemical → electrical energy)
- Hans Christian Ørsted - Electricity produces magnetism
- André-Marie Ampère - Mathematical laws of electromagnetism
- Michael Faraday - Electromagnetic induction, field concept, lines of force
- James Clerk Maxwell - *A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism* (unification, EM waves)
- Heinrich Hertz - Experimental confirmation of EM waves

**Mathematics - New Structures:**
- Carl Friedrich Gauss - Number theory, non-Euclidean geometry, differential geometry
- Bernhard Riemann - Non-Euclidean geometry, complex analysis, Riemann hypothesis
- Évariste Galois - Group theory
- Georg Cantor - Set theory, transfinite numbers (1870s-1880s)

**Biology & Evolution:**
- Gregor Mendel - *Experiments on Plant Hybridization* (1866, rediscovered 1900)
- Charles Darwin - *On the Origin of Species* (1859)

**Practical Components:**
- Building simple circuits (Ohm's law, series/parallel)
- Voltaic cell construction
- Understanding how electricity is generated from analog forces
- What is charge? Motion of charged particles
- Conductivity and electrolysis experiments
- Practice with non-Euclidean geometry concepts
- Introduction to group theory and abstract algebra

---

### 7. Modern Era Part I - Foundations (1900-1945)

**Mathematics - Foundations & Logic:**
- David Hilbert - Formalism, axiomatization programs
- Gottlob Frege - *Begriffsschrift* (formal logic, quantifiers)
- Bertrand Russell & Alfred North Whitehead - *Principia Mathematica* (logic foundations of mathematics)
- Kurt Gödel - Incompleteness theorems (1931) - limits of formal systems

**Modern Physics - Relativity:**
- Albert Einstein - Special Relativity papers (1905), General Relativity (1915)
- Hermann Minkowski - Minkowski spacetime
- Non-Euclidean geometry becomes physically real

**Modern Physics - Quantum Foundations:**
- Max Planck - Quantum hypothesis, lectures on theoretical physics
- Albert Einstein - Photoelectric effect (photons)
- Niels Bohr - Atomic model, Copenhagen interpretation
- Louis de Broglie - Wave theory of matter
- Werner Heisenberg - Uncertainty principle, matrix mechanics
- Erwin Schrödinger - Wave equation, *What Is Life?* (1944 - bridges physics and biology)
- Max Born - Probability interpretation
- Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen - EPR paradox
- Multiple interpretations discussion (Copenhagen, many-worlds, pilot wave)

**Early Computation Concepts:**
- Charles Babbage - Analytical Engine designs (1830s-40s)
- Ada Lovelace - Notes on the Analytical Engine (first algorithm)
- George Boole - *The Laws of Thought* (1854) - Boolean algebra

**Philosophy:**
- Friedrich Nietzsche - *Beyond Good and Evil*, *Genealogy of Morals* (perspectivism, genealogy)
- Max Weber - *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*, essays on bureaucracy and rationalization
- (Other late 19th/early 20th century philosophy as appropriate)

**Practical Components:**
- Introduction to multivariable calculus and PDEs
- Working through special relativity problems
- Basic quantum mechanics problems
- Understanding probability amplitude vs classical probability
- Boolean algebra exercises

---

### 8. Modern Era Part II - Information Age (1945-present)

**Computation & Information Theory:**
- Alan Turing - "On Computable Numbers" (1936) - Turing machines, computability
- Alonzo Church - Lambda calculus (1936)
- John von Neumann - Stored-program computer architecture (1945)
- Claude Shannon - "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" (1948) - information theory, entropy
- Shannon - "A Symbolic Analysis of Relay and Switching Circuits" (1937) - Boolean algebra for circuits

**Computer Science Foundations:**
- Understanding Turing completeness
- Church-Turing thesis
- Halting problem
- Binary encoding/decoding practice
- Machine code and assembly language (pen and paper exercises - "Ancient Greek for computers")
- Digital logic - gates, flip-flops, registers
- Computer architecture - CPU, memory, I/O, von Neumann architecture
- Operating systems basics
- Data structures and algorithms in modern language (Python, Java, or C++)
- Computational complexity theory

**Modern Philosophy:**
- Martin Heidegger - *Being and Time* (selections), *The Question Concerning Technology*
- Ludwig Wittgenstein - *Philosophical Investigations* (language games, meaning as use)
- Karl Popper - *Logic of Scientific Discovery* (falsification)
- Willard Van Orman Quine - "Two Dogmas of Empiricism"
- Albert Camus - *The Myth of Sisyphus* or *The Rebel*

**Philosophy of Mind & AI:**
- Alan Turing - "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" (Turing test)
- John Searle - "Minds, Brains, and Programs" (Chinese Room)
- Thomas Nagel - "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?"
- David Chalmers - Hard problem of consciousness

**Electronics & Practical Computing:**
- Digital-to-analog conversion (DAC/ADC)
- Basic electronics components (resistors, capacitors, inductors, diodes, transistors)
- Building digital logic gates from transistors
- Hands-on projects: Building ADC converter, Arduino/Raspberry Pi projects
- Systems programming exercises
- Introduction to neural networks and machine learning concepts

**Modern Biology (Survey):**
- DNA structure (Watson & Crick, with acknowledgment of Rosalind Franklin)
- Molecular biology - proteins, central dogma
- Genotype and phenotype
- Phylogenetics (Woese)

**Contemporary Culture & Primary Documents:**
- Screenplays exploring philosophical themes:
  - Charlie Chaplin - *Modern Times*
  - Frank Capra - *It's a Wonderful Life*, *Mr. Smith Goes to Washington*
  - Orson Welles - *Citizen Kane*
  - Stanley Kubrick - *2001: A Space Odyssey*, *Dr. Strangelove*
  - *Blade Runner* (Philip K. Dick's *Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?*)
- Important speeches and documents (Lincoln, FDR, Churchill, MLK, etc.)
- UN Declaration of Human Rights

**Post-Hegelian Political Thought (Challenging, Minimal Editorializing):**
- G.W.F. Hegel - Dialectic, master-slave, philosophy of history (selections)
- Karl Marx - Selections on ideology, material conditions
- Carl Schmitt - Selections on authoritarianism
- Hannah Arendt - *The Human Condition*, selections from *Origins of Totalitarianism*
- James Baldwin - See if essays/selections are available
- Simone de Beauvoir - Writings
- Primary political documents from 20th century

**Memetics & Information as Fundamental:**
- How information evolved from vague concept to physical quantity (Shannon)
- DNA as information storage
- Digital age and transformation of society
- (This thread ties together biology, computation, and social change)

## Practical Skills Spine

The unifying thread keeping students oriented and building employable skills:

1. **Ancient-Medieval:** Euclidean proofs, algebraic manipulation, geometric constructions
2. **Early Modern:** Calculus by hand, physical experiments, derivations from first principles
3. **19th Century:** Advanced mathematics, electrical experiments, chemical demonstrations
4. **20th Century - Early:** Mathematical physics problems, Boolean algebra
5. **20th Century - Late:**
   - Binary/machine code exercises (pen and paper)
   - Assembly language programming
   - Data structures and algorithms in modern language
   - Systems programming
   - Building electronics projects (ADC, microcontrollers)
   - Introduction to ML/neural networks

**Capstone:** Student is prepared for technical employment or specialized further study

## Supplementary Materials Needed

### Types of Supplements:
1. **Minimal context notes** - "Why we're reading this now"
2. **Study guides** - Questions to consider, key concepts
3. **Exercise sets** - Math problems, proofs, coding challenges
4. **Lab instructions** - Step-by-step for physical experiments
5. **Translation guides** - Ancient Greek grammar, archaic mathematical notation
6. **Notation bridges** - E.g., Newton's geometric calculus → modern calculus notation

### When to Supplement:
- Original text is genuinely opaque for practice (e.g., Descartes for modern algebra)
- Experiments need safety/procurement guidance
- Translation from archaic notation necessary
- NOT to impose interpretation on texts

## Organizational Principles

**Sequencing:** Primarily chronological (concepts build historically), with flexibility to break for:
- Conceptual threads (optics, atomic theory, computation)
- Juxtaposition for reflection (e.g., Rousseau among Enlightenment optimism)

**Depth vs. Breadth:**
- Most texts: Key selections, not complete works
- Some texts: Nearly complete (Euclid's *Elements*, Newton's *Principia*)

**Pacing:**
- Grand Tour: 2-3 years full-time equivalent
- Focused Tracks: 6-12 months
- Quick Dives: 1-2 months

**Assessment:**
- Self-graded exercises
- Progress tracking
- Community discussion for deeper understanding
- No formal grades, but clear milestones

---

## Community & Cohort Model

- **Self-paced but coordinated:** Track individual progress, form cohorts with similar pace
- **Discussion groups:** Weekly/biweekly sessions (Discord/Slack initially)
- **Structured prompts:** Guide discussion without imposing interpretation
- **Shared experience:** Reading Newton alone is hard; with others transforms it
